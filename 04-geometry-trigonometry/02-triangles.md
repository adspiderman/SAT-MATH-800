# Lesson 4.2 — Triangles: Similarity & Congruence

## Concepts

### Angle facts

- Interior angles sum to **180°**.
- **Exterior angle = sum of the two remote interior angles.** (Faster than computing the third angle first.)
- Isosceles: two equal sides ⇔ the angles opposite them are equal. Equilateral: all 60°.
- Larger side is opposite larger angle.

### Triangle inequality

Each side is less than the sum and greater than the difference of the other two. Sides 5 and 9 → third side strictly between 4 and 14. "How many integer lengths possible?" → 5–13 → **9 values**.

### Similar triangles — the SAT's favorite geometry tool

Two triangles with two equal angle pairs (AA) are similar: corresponding sides are **proportional**.

**Where similarity hides:**
- A line parallel to one side of a triangle cuts off a smaller similar triangle.
- Two triangles sharing vertical angles between parallel lines ("bowtie" / X configuration).
- The altitude to the hypotenuse of a right triangle creates three similar triangles.

**Worked example.** In triangle ABC, DE ∥ BC with D on AB, E on AC. AD = 4, DB = 6, DE = 5. Find BC.
- △ADE ~ △ABC with ratio AD/AB = 4/10. So DE/BC = 4/10 → BC = 5 · 10/4 = **12.5**.

**Set up ratios by corresponding vertices** (small triangle side / matching big triangle side), never by "left over right" guessing.

### Congruence (equal triangles)

SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS make triangles congruent (all parts equal). The SAT uses congruence lightly — mostly to justify equal lengths in figures. (SSA is NOT a congruence rule.)

### Area

A = ½ · base · height, where height ⊥ base. On the coordinate plane, pick a horizontal/vertical side as the base when possible.

## Traps

1. **Ratio direction:** small/big consistently. Mixing DE/BC with AB/AD inverts the answer — and that inverse is an answer choice.
2. The parallel-line cutoff: AD/AB = 4/**10** (whole side), not 4/6 (the two pieces). DB is not a side of the small triangle.
3. Exterior angle = remote interior sum — not supplementary to the wrong angle.
4. Perimeter scales by k, area by **k²** in similar figures.
5. "Isosceles" without saying which sides are equal → possibly two cases; check both against the answer choices.

## Practice Set

1. Two angles of a triangle are 47° and 71°. Find the third.
2. An exterior angle at C of triangle ABC measures 130°, and angle A = 55°. Find angle B.
3. A triangle has sides 5 and 9. How many integer values are possible for the third side?
4. Triangle ABC is isosceles with AB = AC and angle A = 40°. Find angle B.
5. In triangle ABC, DE ∥ BC, AD = 4, DB = 6, DE = 5. Find BC.
6. Triangles PQR and XYZ are similar with PQ = 6, XY = 9. If the area of PQR is 20, find the area of XYZ.
7. A 6-ft person casts a 4-ft shadow while a tree casts a 30-ft shadow. How tall is the tree?
8. Right triangle with legs 9 and 12: what is the length of the altitude to the hypotenuse?
9. The vertices of a triangle are (0,0), (8,0), (3,7). What is its area?
10. In the "bowtie" configuration, AB ∥ CD, and diagonals AC and BD meet at E. AE = 3, EC = 9, BE = 4. Find ED.

### Answers

1. **62°**.
2. **75°** — 130 = 55 + B.
3. **9** — integers 5 through 13.
4. **70°** — (180 − 40)/2.
5. **12.5** — ratio 4:10.
6. **45** — ratio 3/2, area ratio 9/4; 20 × 9/4 = 45.
7. **45 ft** — 6/4 = h/30.
8. **7.2** — hypotenuse 15; area = ½(9)(12) = 54 = ½(15)h → h = 108/15 = 7.2.
9. **28** — base 8, height 7.
10. **12** — △ABE ~ △CDE, ratio 3:9 = 1:3 → ED = 3 × BE = 12.
