# Lesson 3.7 — Study Design & Inference

## Concepts

No calculations here — pure logic. 1–2 questions per test, and they're free points once you know four ideas.

### Idea 1 — Random SAMPLING → generalize to the population

A conclusion about a population is valid only if the sample was selected **at random from that population**.

- Survey random students *at one school* → conclusions apply to **that school only**, not "all teenagers."
- Volunteers, mall shoppers, magazine respondents → **biased sample** → no valid generalization.
- The population you may generalize to = exactly the population that was randomly sampled. Nothing broader.

### Idea 2 — Random ASSIGNMENT → cause and effect

To claim a treatment *causes* an outcome, subjects must be **randomly assigned** to treatment/control groups.

- Observational study (people chose their own behavior) → association only, never causation.
- Randomized experiment → causation is a valid conclusion.

| Design | Random sampling? | Random assignment? | Valid conclusion |
|--------|------------------|--------------------|------------------|
| Both | ✓ | ✓ | cause & effect, generalizable to population |
| Experiment on volunteers | ✗ | ✓ | cause & effect, but only for those like the participants |
| Random survey | ✓ | ✗ | association in the population, no causation |
| Neither | ✗ | ✗ | association among the subjects only |

### Idea 3 — Margin of error

A survey estimate ± margin of error gives a **plausible range** for the true population value.
- "Mean 24 minutes, margin of error 3" → the true population mean is plausibly between **21 and 27**.
- **Larger sample → smaller margin of error** (more precision). Margin of error doesn't fix bias — a bigger biased sample is still biased.
- It describes uncertainty about the *population value*, not the spread of individuals.

### Idea 4 — Correct SAT answer style

The correct choice is usually the **most cautious** one that stays within the sampled population and avoids causal words (unless a randomized experiment). Wrong answers overreach: bigger population, causal claims, exact values instead of estimates.

## Traps

1. Big sample ≠ good sample. 10,000 volunteers is worse than 500 random.
2. "Correlation between X and Y" never proves X causes Y — there may be lurking variables.
3. Margin of error applies to the population estimate, not to any individual.
4. Generalize only to the population actually sampled — school ≠ city ≠ country.

## Practice Set

1. A researcher randomly surveys 300 students at Lincoln High and finds 60% support later start times. To which group can this be generalized?
2. In #1, can we conclude later start times *cause* anything? Why or why not?
3. A study randomly assigns 200 volunteers to drug or placebo; the drug group improves significantly. What conclusion is valid?
4. A poll estimates a candidate's support at 52% with a margin of error of 4%. What is the plausible range of true support?
5. Which change most reduces margin of error: (A) increasing sample size, (B) surveying volunteers instead, (C) asking more questions?
6. A fitness magazine surveys its readers and finds 80% exercise daily. Why can't this generalize to all adults?
7. An observational study finds coffee drinkers have higher productivity. A news headline says "Coffee makes you productive." What's wrong?
8. A sample of 25 has margin of error 6; a sample of 400 from the same population would have a margin of error that is (larger/smaller)?
9. Researchers want to estimate the mean commute time in a city of 500,000. Describe the sampling method required.
10. In a randomized experiment on 150 randomly selected patients from a clinic, the treatment group healed faster. What is the strongest valid conclusion?

### Answers

1. **Students at Lincoln High only** — that was the sampled population.
2. **No** — it's a survey (no random assignment); only an association/opinion estimate.
3. **The drug caused improvement among people similar to the volunteers** — random assignment gives causation; volunteer sample limits generalization.
4. **48% to 56%**.
5. **A** — larger random samples shrink the margin of error.
6. **Self-selected, non-random sample** — readers of a fitness magazine differ systematically from all adults.
7. **Causation claimed from an observational study** — coffee drinkers may differ in other ways.
8. **Smaller**.
9. **Select residents at random from the entire city** (e.g., a simple random sample of all residents).
10. **The treatment causes faster healing for patients in that clinic's population** — both randomizations present.
